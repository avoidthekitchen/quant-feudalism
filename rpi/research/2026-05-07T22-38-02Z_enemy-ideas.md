# Enemy Ideas: Ranged Arena Enemies

Created: 2026-05-07T22:38:02Z

Priority order. Enemy 1 is recommended for implementation first.

---

## 1. Hopper (Telegraphed Charged Shot)

A ranged enemy that maintains distance from the player via erratic hops and fires telegraphed charged shots. Forces the player to use Dash offensively to close the gap.

### Spawning

- Appears on even arena rounds (round 2, 4, 6, ...)
- 1 hopper per qualifying round (so round 2 has 1 hopper, round 4 has 2, etc.)
- Spawns at a random position at least 300px from the player

### Core Movement: Hopping

The hopper does not walk. It moves exclusively via hops.

**Hop behavior:**
- **Telegraph:** 0.2s windup flash (yellow tint, slight crouch/size change)
- **Hop distance:** 150-200px in the chosen direction
- **Hop duration:** ~0.18s (fast, snappy - similar feel to drone lunge)
- **Hop speed:** ~900-1100 px/s for the duration (covers 150-200px)
- **Hop cooldown:** 1.2-1.5s (slightly longer than player dash cooldown of 1.1s)
- **Vulnerability window:** 0.4s after landing where hopper cannot hop again. Briefly tinted white to signal vulnerability.

**Direction selection:**
- If player is within flee trigger distance (~250px), hop **away** from player
- If player is between 250-400px, hop **perpendicular** to player (circling behavior)
- If player is beyond 400px, hop **toward** player to reach preferred range
- Preferred engagement distance: 250-350px

**Corner escape:**
- If the hopper would land within 60px of a wall, it instead hops **past the player** (direction = away from nearest wall, angled to cross behind the player)
- This prevents corner-trapping and keeps the fight mobile

**Erratic feel:**
- Add a random angular offset of +/- 25 degrees to each hop direction
- Vary hop distance by +/- 20% randomly
- Use a slight arc animation (y-scale squash/stretch during hop) to sell the "hopping" feel

### Attack: Charged Shot

**Windup phase (0.5s):**
- Hopper stops moving and cannot hop
- Telegraphed with a bright orange/red glow and a growing energy circle
- A thin line is drawn from hopper toward the player's current position (telegraph direction)

**Firing:**
- Single projectile toward the player's position at the start of windup (not tracking)
- Projectile speed: ~350 px/s (slow enough to react to, fast enough to pressure)
- Projectile damage: 18
- Projectile hit radius: 20px
- Shot cooldown: 2.0s (independent of hop cooldown)

**Player counterplay:**
- The 0.5s windup is the vulnerability window. Player can:
  - Dash in and melee to punish (dash covers ~113px + melee range 166px = 279px closing power)
  - Sidestep the shot and reposition
  - Use ranged attack (Bolt) during the windup
- If the player dashes in during windup, they arrive during or just after the shot fires, with enough time to melee during the 0.4s post-hop vulnerability window

### Stats

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| HP | 36 (lower than drone's 44 since it's harder to reach) |
| Touch damage | 10 (less than drone's 14 - it's a ranged unit) |
| Preferred range | 250-350px |
| Flee trigger | 250px |
| Hop cooldown | 1.2-1.5s |
| Shot cooldown | 2.0s |
| Shot damage | 18 |
| Shot speed | 350 px/s |

### Visual Design Notes

- Smaller sprite than the drone (suggests fragility)
- Brighter/more vibrant color to distinguish from drones at a glance
- Squash/stretch on hop (compress vertically before hop, stretch during hop, bounce on land)
- Orange/red glow during shot windup contrasts with drone's yellow lunge telegraph

## 2. Grappler (Aggro Pull Mechanic)

A ranged enemy that reverses the chase dynamic. Instead of the player choosing when to engage, the grappler pulls the player in and then hops away, forcing reactive play.

### Spawning

- Alternate with hoppers on different even rounds, or appear on round 6+ as an additional enemy type
- Suggest: hoppers on rounds 2, 6, 10, ... and grapplers on rounds 4, 8, 12, ...

### Core Mechanic: Gravity Hook

The grappler fires a tether projectile that, on hit, **pulls the player toward the grappler** rather than dealing damage.

**Gravity Hook:**
- Projectile speed: ~500 px/s (moderately fast)
- Telegraph: 0.35s charge-up with a visible energy tendril growing toward the player
- On hit: player receives a velocity impulse of ~600 px/s toward the grappler (not a teleport - physics-based pull)
- Player can still dash during the pull to redirect or cancel momentum
- Pull does no damage, but positions the player near the grappler
- Hook cooldown: 2.5s

### Follow-up: Scatter Shot

After a successful pull, the grappler immediately fires a **spread of 3 projectiles** in a cone aimed at the pulled player:

- 3 projectiles in a 40-degree cone
- Each deals 12 damage (potential 36 if all hit)
- Projectile speed: ~300 px/s
- The grappler then hops away (200px, ~0.2s duration)
- This creates the core loop: pull → spread shot → hop away → player chases → pull again

### Evasion Behavior

- Between hook cooldowns, the grappler slowly drifts away from the player at ~80 px/s (much slower than hopper)
- If the player dashes toward the grappler, it performs a **reactive dodge hop**:
  - 120px perpendicular to the dash direction
  - 0.4s cooldown before it can dodge again
  - Telegraphed with a brief crouch (0.15s)
- This means the player can't just dash straight at it - they need to dash at an angle or bait the dodge first

### Player Counterplay

- **Dash to redirect the pull:** When hooked, dash perpendicular to the grappler. The dash speed (780 px/s) overpowers the pull impulse (600 px/s), letting the player escape the spread shot cone
- **Bait the hook, then close:** Intentionally stay at range to trigger the hook, dash during the 0.35s telegraph to close distance, melee the grappler before it can fire the spread shot
- **Punish after dodge:** Let the grappler waste its reactive dodge, then dash in during the 0.4s dodge cooldown

### Stats

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| HP | 40 |
| Touch damage | 8 |
| Preferred range | 300-400px |
| Hook cooldown | 2.5s |
| Hook pull force | 600 px/s impulse |
| Spread shot damage | 12 per projectile (3 projectiles) |
| Reactive dodge cooldown | 0.4s |
| Drift speed | 80 px/s |

### Visual Design Notes

- Tendril/whip-like telegraph for the hook (not a line - something organic)
- Purple/violet color scheme to contrast with the hopper's warmer tones
- Player gets a brief visual tether line during the pull
- Grappler "recoils" when firing spread shot, telegraphing the hop that follows

### Differentiation from Hopper

| | Hopper | Grappler |
|---|---|---|
| Initiative | Player chooses when to engage | Grappler forces engagement timing |
| Pressure type | Steady ranged poke with repositioning | Burst window (pull + spread) |
| Player skill tested | Dash timing to close distance | Dash timing to redirect/reposition |
| Counterplay rhythm | Wait for vulnerability window, dash in | Read the hook telegraph, respond correctly |
| Threat when ignored | Constant chip damage from charged shots | Burst damage from pull + spread combo |

## 3. Sower (Hopping Mine Layer)

A ranged enemy that gradually fills the arena with persistent hazards. Creates escalating time pressure - the longer the fight goes, the more dangerous the arena becomes.

### Spawning

- Appears on round 8+ (later introduction since it fundamentally changes the arena)
- 1 sower per qualifying round, capped at 2 sowers alive at once

### Core Mechanic: Mine Deployment

The sower hops around the arena (similar to the hopper's movement) and drops a **hazard zone** at each landing point.

**Hazard zone properties:**
- Radius: 60px
- Duration: 8 seconds, then fades over 1 second (total life: 9s)
- Effect: deals 8 damage per 0.5s tick to the player while standing in it
- Visual: glowing crack/void patch on the ground, expanding slightly during the fade-in
- Maximum active mines per sower: 6 (oldest mine despawns when limit exceeded)

**Hop behavior:**
- Hops every 1.0-1.3s (faster than hopper since hops are its primary action)
- Hop distance: 180-240px (slightly longer than hopper to spread mines across the arena)
- Does not flee from the player - hops semi-randomly across the arena
- Avoids hopping onto existing hazard zones (prevents stacking)
- 0.25s vulnerability window after landing

### Attack: Seed Shot

Occasionally (every 3.0s), the sower fires a projectile that creates a hazard zone at the impact point:

- Projectile speed: ~280 px/s (slow, arcing)
- Telegraph: 0.3s windup
- Targeting: aims at the player's current position
- On impact: creates a hazard zone (same properties as dropped mines)
- This lets the sower place hazards near the player even when it's hopping elsewhere

### Escalation Design

The sower's threat is unique: **it doesn't directly kill the player, it shrinks the safe space.**

- Round 8 with 1 sower: player has plenty of room to fight drones and avoid mines
- Round 10 with 1 sower + more drones: mines are more restrictive since the player is dodging drones
- Round 12+ with 2 sowers: arena is filling up fast, forcing aggressive play to eliminate sowers
- Hazard zones from dead sowers persist until they expire naturally

### Player Counterplay

- **Prioritize the sower early:** Kill it before the arena fills up, even if drones are closer
- **Bait hops toward walls:** The sower's semi-random hops can be influenced by player positioning - standing near the center pushes mines outward
- **Dash through hazard zones:** Dash invulnerability (0.24s) covers crossing a 60px zone at dash speed
- **Use ranged attacks (Bolt):** The sower's slower hop speed and lack of evasion make it vulnerable to ranged attacks, rewarding the player for using both attack types

### Stats

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| HP | 30 (lowest HP - it's a priority target, not a tank) |
| Touch damage | 6 |
| Hop interval | 1.0-1.3s |
| Hop distance | 180-240px |
| Mine damage | 8 per 0.5s tick |
| Mine radius | 60px |
| Mine duration | 8s + 1s fade |
| Max active mines | 6 per sower |
| Seed shot cooldown | 3.0s |
| Seed shot damage | 0 (creates mine, no direct damage) |

### Visual Design Notes

- Green/acid color scheme (hazard zones = toxic/corrupt terrain)
- Sower leaves a brief trail/particles during hops
- Hazard zones pulse subtly and have a different visual from the arena floor
- Sound design: distinct "planting" sound on each hop landing

### Differentiation from Hopper & Grappler

| | Hopper | Grappler | Sower |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary threat | Direct damage | Burst combo | Arena control |
| Time pressure | Per-engagement (vulnerability windows) | Per-engagement (hook timing) | Per-round (arena fills up) |
| Player response | Dash in, melee | React to hook, reposition | Prioritize target, manage space |
| Synergy with drones | Distraction from drones | Forces player into drone positions | Restricts drone-fighting space |
| Skill tested | Timing | Reading telegraphs | Target prioritization |

## 4. Wraith (Phase-Shifting Enemy)

An elusive enemy that alternates between visible (vulnerable) and phased (invulnerable) states. Creates a hide-and-seek rhythm where the player must track the enemy and strike during brief vulnerability windows.

### Spawning

- Appears on round 10+ (latest introduction - requires the player to already be comfortable with the arena)
- 1 wraith per qualifying round, capped at 2 wraiths alive at once

### Core Mechanic: Phase Shift Cycle

The wraith cycles between two states:

**Materialized state (visible, vulnerable):**
- Duration: 2.5s
- Visible with a solid sprite and full collision
- Can take damage and be stunned
- Moves slowly toward the player at ~100 px/s
- Tinted with a steady glow (e.g., cool blue)
- Flickers 0.3s before shifting to phased state (telegraph)

**Phased state (invisible, invulnerable):**
- Duration: 2.0s
- Sprite becomes semi-transparent (alpha ~0.15) and loses collision
- Cannot be damaged or targeted by melee/ranged
- Moves toward the player at ~180 px/s (faster while phased)
- Leaves faint particle trail (directional hint for the player)
- Player can still hear subtle audio cues (ethereal hum that gets louder as wraith gets closer)
- Flickers 0.3s before materializing (telegraph)

### Attack: Phase Strike

The wraith can only attack during the transition from phased → materialized state:

- When materializing, if within 120px of the player, it performs a **phase strike**
- Telegraph: 0.3s materialization animation (sprite fades in with a slash windup)
- Strike deals 22 damage in a narrow cone (30-degree half-angle, 120px range)
- If outside 120px, it just materializes and resumes slow approach
- This incentivizes the wraith to close distance during the phased state, and the player to create distance when they see the flicker

### Evasion During Materialized State

While visible and vulnerable, the wraith tries to position itself optimally:

- If the player dashes toward it, the wraith performs a **ghost dash**: quick sidestep of ~80px perpendicular to the dash direction
- Ghost dash cooldown: 1.5s (can be baited)
- If the player uses a ranged attack (Bolt), the wraith phases early (truncates its materialized window by up to 0.8s)
- This makes ranged attacks less effective against the wraith - the player is incentivized to use melee

### Player Counterplay

- **Track the particle trail:** Watch the faint trail during phased state to predict where the wraith will materialize. Pre-position and dash in when it flickers
- **Bait the ghost dash:** Dash toward the wraith to trigger its sidestep, then dash again (if available) or walk to the new position and melee during the 1.5s cooldown
- **Create distance on flicker:** When the wraith flickers before phasing, move away so it can't phase strike on re-materialization
- **Punish materialization:** The 2.5s materialized window is the only time to deal damage. Time the dash + melee for the start of the window, before the wraith can ghost dash

### Stats

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| HP | 32 (low - balanced by difficulty of hitting it) |
| Touch damage | 6 (while materialized) |
| Phase strike damage | 22 |
| Materialized duration | 2.5s |
| Phased duration | 2.0s |
| Materialized speed | 100 px/s |
| Phased speed | 180 px/s |
| Ghost dash distance | 80px |
| Ghost dash cooldown | 1.5s |
| Phase strike range | 120px |
| Phase strike cone | 30-degree half-angle |

### Visual Design Notes

- Ethereal/ghostly appearance - flowing edges, slight transparency even when materialized
- Cool blue/silver color scheme
- Particle trail during phased state: small, faint, dissipating orbs moving in the direction of travel
- Materialization: sprite "condenses" from transparent to solid with a brief flash
- Phase strike: a wide arc slash with a ghostly afterimage

### Differentiation from Other Enemies

| | Hopper | Grappler | Sower | Wraith |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Core loop | Hop → shoot → hop | Pull → spread → dodge | Hop → mine → hop | Phase → position → strike → phase |
| Vulnerability | Post-hop window | During hook windup | Always (but low priority) | Materialized state only |
| Player skill tested | Dash timing | Dash redirection | Target prioritization | Tracking + reaction |
| Information game | None (everything visible) | Read telegraph direction | Manage spatial awareness | Track invisible movement |
| Best counter | Dash + melee | Dash perpendicular | Ranged attacks + focus fire | Melee during materialize |

## Implementation Priority

1. **Hopper** - Most straightforward, introduces ranged threat without new mechanics (projectiles already exist). Minimal new systems needed.
2. **Grappler** - Builds on hopper's hop system, adds pull mechanic. Requires a new projectile type and player velocity impulse system.
3. **Sower** - Requires a persistent hazard zone system (new). Adds arena-control gameplay that scales with round count.
4. **Wraith** - Most complex. Requires phase state machine, invisibility, particle trail system, and audio cues. Latest introduction gives more time to implement.

## Naming

The existing chase-and-lunge enemy is called a **Bug** (per `CONTEXT.md`). "Drone" is explicitly listed as a term to avoid. The internal code still uses `drone` but the domain language is Bug.

Each new enemy type gets its own distinct name (Hopper, Grappler, Sower, Wraith). "Bug" refers specifically to the original melee enemy.

## Spawn System: Weighted Random Pool

Instead of a fixed schedule, each arena round randomly selects enemy types from a weighted pool. This keeps runs feeling different while controlling complexity.

### Constraints

1. **Round 1 is always Bugs only.** The player needs a clean learning environment before new types appear.
2. **Minimum unlock round per type:**
   - Bug: round 1+
   - Hopper: round 2+
   - Grappler: round 4+
   - Sower: round 8+
   - Wraith: round 10+
3. **Alive-at-once caps** prevent degenerate scenarios:
   - Bug: no cap (they're the baseline enemy)
   - Hopper: no cap
   - Grappler: max 2 alive
   - Sower: max 2 alive
   - Wraith: max 2 alive
4. **Total enemy count still follows the existing formula:** `min(spawnPositions.length, 5 + roundsFinished)`. The randomization only affects which types fill those slots.

### Weighting Curve

Each round, the new enemy slots (beyond the existing Bug population) are filled by rolling against this weight table. Weights are relative within a round — the system picks from all unlocked types proportionally.

| Round | Bug | Hopper | Grappler | Sower | Wraith | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 100% | — | — | — | — | Bugs only |
| 2-3 | 70% | 30% | — | — | — | Gentle introduction of Hopper |
| 4-5 | 50% | 30% | 20% | — | — | Grappler enters at lower weight |
| 6-7 | 40% | 25% | 25% | — | — | Hopper and Grappler equalize |
| 8-9 | 30% | 20% | 20% | 15% | — | Sower enters; Bug majority drops |
| 10-11 | 25% | 20% | 20% | 15% | 10% | Wraith enters at low weight |
| 12-13 | 20% | 20% | 20% | 15% | 15% | Wraith ramps up |
| 14+ | 15% | 20% | 20% | 20% | 20% | Near-equal distribution; Bug is minority |

### How It Works

For each non-Bug slot in the round's enemy count:

1. Collect all unlocked enemy types and their weights for the current round
2. Roll a weighted random selection
3. Check the alive-at-once cap for the selected type
4. If capped, exclude that type and re-roll from remaining options
5. If all non-Bug types are capped, fill the slot with a Bug instead

This means early rounds are mostly Bugs with occasional variety, and later rounds have a rich mix. The caps prevent scenarios like 5 Sowers filling the arena with mines.

### Implementation Sketch

```typescript
type EnemyType = "bug" | "hopper" | "grappler" | "sower" | "wraith";

const ENEMY_UNLOCK_ROUND: Record<EnemyType, number> = {
  bug: 1,
  hopper: 2,
  grappler: 4,
  sower: 8,
  wraith: 10,
};

const ENEMY_ALIVE_CAP: Partial<Record<EnemyType, number>> = {
  grappler: 2,
  sower: 2,
  wraith: 2,
};

function getSpawnWeights(round: number): Record<EnemyType, number> {
  if (round <= 1) return { bug: 1, hopper: 0, grappler: 0, sower: 0, wraith: 0 };
  if (round <= 3) return { bug: 0.70, hopper: 0.30, grappler: 0, sower: 0, wraith: 0 };
  if (round <= 5) return { bug: 0.50, hopper: 0.30, grappler: 0.20, sower: 0, wraith: 0 };
  if (round <= 7) return { bug: 0.40, hopper: 0.25, grappler: 0.25, sower: 0, wraith: 0 };
  if (round <= 9) return { bug: 0.30, hopper: 0.20, grappler: 0.20, sower: 0.15, wraith: 0 };
  if (round <= 11) return { bug: 0.25, hopper: 0.20, grappler: 0.20, sower: 0.15, wraith: 0.10 };
  if (round <= 13) return { bug: 0.20, hopper: 0.20, grappler: 0.20, sower: 0.15, wraith: 0.15 };
  return { bug: 0.15, hopper: 0.20, grappler: 0.20, sower: 0.20, wraith: 0.20 };
}

function pickEnemyType(
  round: number,
  aliveCounts: Record<EnemyType, number>,
): EnemyType {
  const weights = getSpawnWeights(round);
  const candidates = (Object.keys(weights) as EnemyType[]).filter((type) => {
    if (weights[type] <= 0) return false;
    if (round < ENEMY_UNLOCK_ROUND[type]) return false;
    const cap = ENEMY_ALIVE_CAP[type];
    if (cap !== undefined && aliveCounts[type] >= cap) return false;
    return true;
  });
  if (candidates.length === 0) return "bug";
  const totalWeight = candidates.reduce((sum, t) => sum + weights[t], 0);
  let roll = Math.random() * totalWeight;
  for (const type of candidates) {
    roll -= weights[type];
    if (roll <= 0) return type;
  }
  return candidates[candidates.length - 1];
}
```

### Why Weighted Random Over Fixed Schedule

- **Replayability:** Two runs at round 6 can feel very different (mostly Bugs vs. a Grappler-heavy mix)
- **Adaptability test:** The player can't memorize a spawn order — they must read each round's composition and adjust strategy
- **Smooth gating:** The unlock rounds and weight curve prevent overwhelming the player while gradually increasing variety
- **Caps prevent degeneracy:** Even with randomness, the worst case is bounded

## Per-Round HP Scaling

All enemy types receive a global HP multiplier that increases with each round completed. This creates a coherent difficulty curve that runs parallel to the existing `combatSpeedMultiplier` and shop price growth (`SHOP_BUNDLE_COST_GROWTH_PER_ROUND = 1.075`).

### Formula

```
hpMultiplier = 1 + 0.06 * roundsFinished
```

Applied uniformly: `actualHP = Math.round(baseHP * hpMultiplier)`

### Scaling Table

| Round | Multiplier | Bug (44) | Hopper (36) | Grappler (40) | Sower (30) | Wraith (32) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1.00 | 44 | — | — | — | — |
| 2 | 1.12 | 49 | 40 | — | — | — |
| 4 | 1.24 | 55 | 45 | 50 | — | — |
| 6 | 1.36 | 60 | 49 | 54 | — | — |
| 8 | 1.48 | 65 | 53 | 59 | 44 | — |
| 10 | 1.60 | 70 | 58 | 64 | 48 | 51 |
| 15 | 1.90 | 84 | 68 | 76 | 57 | 61 |
| 20 | 2.20 | 97 | 79 | 88 | 66 | 70 |

### Why a Multiplier Over Flat Increases

- **Preserves kill-combo readability.** At round 1, Bug takes 2 Slashes (23 * 2 = 46 > 44). Flat increases break this — a round 5 Bug with +3/round becomes 59 HP, which takes 3 Slashes for no clear reason. A multiplier stretches all HP proportionally, so the player perceives "enemies are tougher" rather than "this specific enemy suddenly needs an extra hit."
- **Works for all types without per-type tuning.** One formula covers everything. No need to decide whether Hopper should get +2 or +3 HP per round.
- **Player upgrades naturally outpace it.** The player acquires better cards, more copies, and compute discounts through the shop. A 6% per-round increase is slow enough that a well-equipped player barely notices, while an under-equipped player feels the squeeze.
- **Coherent with existing scaling.** The game already scales difficulty via:
  - `combatSpeedMultiplier` (1.5x) — global speed
  - `SHOP_BUNDLE_COST_GROWTH_PER_ROUND` (1.075x) — shop price inflation
  - Enemy count (`5 + roundsFinished`) — more enemies
  - HP scaling adds a fourth axis that doesn't require rebalancing the others.

### Interaction with Enemy Count

Total enemy HP on the field scales from two sources: more enemies *and* tougher enemies. Combined:

| Round | Enemies | Bug HP Each | Total Bug HP | vs Round 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 5 | 44 | 220 | 1.0x |
| 5 | 10 | 57 | 570 | 2.6x |
| 10 | 15 | 70 | 1050 | 4.8x |
| 15 | 20 | 84 | 1680 | 7.6x |

This is aggressive but sustainable because:
- The player has better cards and more compute by later rounds
- Mixed enemy types include low-HP enemies (Sower at 30 base, Wraith at 32 base)
- The weighted random pool means not all enemies are Bugs
- The player earns more credits per round (`kills * 12 + 36`) to spend on upgrades

### Alternative: Diminishing Returns

If the scaling feels too steep in testing, switch to a diminishing formula:

```
hpMultiplier = 1 + 0.08 * Math.sqrt(roundsFinished)
```

| Round | Multiplier | Bug HP |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1.00 | 44 |
| 5 | 1.18 | 52 |
| 10 | 1.25 | 55 |
| 15 | 1.31 | 58 |
| 20 | 1.36 | 60 |

This keeps early ramp (rounds 1-5) nearly identical but flattens out at higher rounds, letting enemy variety and count carry the difficulty instead. Start with the linear 6% formula and tune down to this if round 15+ feels like a slog.
